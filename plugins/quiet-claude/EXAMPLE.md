Herbert Hoover (1874–1964) is one of the most tragic figures in American 
political history — a man whose reputation was inverted almost completely by 
four years in office.

Before the presidency: the "Great Humanitarian"
Hoover was born in West Branch, Iowa, to a Quaker family, orphaned by age nine. 
He worked his way through Stanford's inaugural class as a geology major and 
became a spectacularly successful mining engineer, making a fortune on projects 
across Australia, China, and Burma. By his early forties he was a self-made 
millionaire.

His fame came from relief work. When World War I broke out, he organized the 
evacuation of stranded Americans from Europe, then ran the Commission for 
Relief in Belgium — feeding roughly nine million people in occupied 
territory, negotiating passage with both the British blockade and the German 
occupiers as essentially a private citizen with no diplomatic standing. He 
later ran the U.S. Food Administration during the war and the American Relief 
Administration afterward, including famine relief in Soviet Russia in 1921–23 
that likely saved millions of lives. He was, plausibly, the most admired 
non-political American alive.

As Commerce Secretary under Harding and Coolidge (1921–28), he transformed a 
sleepy department into an activist agency, pushing standardization, trade 
promotion, radio regulation, and aviation. Colleagues joked he was "Secretary 
of Commerce and Under-Secretary of everything else."

The presidency (1929–33)
He won in a 1928 landslide. The crash came eight months later.

The conventional caricature — that Hoover was a do-nothing ideologue who let 
the economy rot — is wrong in the particulars but captures something real 
about the outcome. He did act, and by prior standards aggressively: the 
Reconstruction Finance Corporation lent to banks and railroads, the Federal 
Home Loan Bank system was created, public works like the Hoover Dam went 
forward, and he pressed businesses to hold wages steady. What he refused was 
direct federal relief to individuals, believing it would corrode self-reliance 
and that voluntarism and local government should carry it. He also signed the 
Smoot-Hawley Tariff in 1930 over the objections of a thousand economists, which 
strangled international trade, and backed a tax increase in 1932 that was 
actively contractionary.

The "Hoovervilles" — shantytowns named in derision — and the 1932 rout of 
the Bonus Army veterans by federal troops under MacArthur sealed the public 
image. He lost to FDR in 1932 by a crushing margin.

Afterward
He lived another 31 years, longer post-presidency than almost anyone, and spent 
them as a bitter critic of the New Deal — then was partly rehabilitated when 
Truman brought him back to organize post-WWII food relief and to chair the 
Hoover Commissions on reorganizing the executive branch.

How to think about him
The most useful reading is that Hoover was a brilliant technocrat whose entire 
worldview — engineering-minded, voluntarist, allergic to federal coercion — 
was well-suited to famine relief and badly mismatched to a systemic collapse of 
demand. He wasn't callous; he was ideologically constrained at exactly the 
moment that constraint was fatal. Historians generally rank him poorly as 
president while conceding he did more than his reputation allows, and that no 
policy toolkit available in 1930 was likely to have been sufficient.

If you want, I can go deeper on any single piece — the Belgian relief 
operation, the specific policy fights of 1930–32, or the Hoover-FDR 
interregnum, which is its own remarkable story.
